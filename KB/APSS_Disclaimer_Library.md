What is a Flying Freehold?
A flying freehold occurs when a part of a freehold property extends over (or under) another separate freehold property, without being directly connected to the ground (e.g., an upper room over a shared passageway or neighbouring building). The affected space remains part of the owner’s property, even though it structurally overlaps with someone else’s land.

Key Considerations for Buyers
-Ownership Clarity
Without clear reference to the flying freehold in the Title Deeds, it may be difficult to define boundaries, and the extent of ownership over the area in question may be disputed. Establishing legal title and proving your rights over the overhanging/underlying space will likely require further investigation or legal input.
-Mortgage & Lending Risk
Many lenders are cautious about properties with flying freeholds—particularly unregistered ones—as they present additional legal complexity and potential enforcement issues. Mortgage approvals may be delayed, restricted, or refused unless supporting documentation is obtained and clarified.
-Neighbour Disputes & Maintenance
Where a property overlaps another, and no clear legal covenants exist, disputes can arise about access for repairs, maintenance responsibilities, or alterations. Enforcing rights may be difficult if legal agreements (such as mutual maintenance covenants or easements) are not already in place.

Recommendations

-Seek independent legal advice before proceeding
-Request further investigation into the flying freehold and unregistered status
-Be aware that mortgage options may be limited
-Consider the risk of delays and added cost during conveyancing
